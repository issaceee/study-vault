
**Summary:**

Gallagher and Greenblatt argue for [[New Historicism]] as a way to reconnect literature to historical contexts of the time, rather than as individual works of art appreciated in Â isolation. They argue that New Historicism requires different academic lenses, as they reject one singular Cultural identity, for there is an impossibility in defining boundaries that segregate a group of people from others, or a time period from another without encountering overlaps in almost every way. Hence, they posit to focus on particulars not as a reflection of universality, but as an expression of a unique personal historical experience. Another key concept is to look at more than just literature, but other texts as well, from high brow art to low brow literature (eg. gossip) as all these would contribute to a more holistic understanding of the historical experience of a particular time.

In order to demonstrate this, Gallagher and Greenblatt chart the development of the symbolism of the potato in the 18th and 19th century as a symbolism of poverty, compared to that of bread, a symbol of the Christian Eucharist. They reveal how the potato, rather than just being a mere object as materialism suggests, begin to adopt various symbolic meanings (poverty, the Other, excess, risk to the political and economic, capitalist structure). Analysing the potato through anthropological, textual and historical evidence, they prove that ideas of spirituality (mortality vs divinity), the economy (poverty, labour value), the human body (hunger, fertility) and politics (structures, state power, moral order), converge in the potato, providing a comprehensive overview of the 18th and 19th century.
