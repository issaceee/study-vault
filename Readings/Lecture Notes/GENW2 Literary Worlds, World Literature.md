## Provincial Worlds: Bankimchandra Chattopadhyay’s *Rajmohan’s Wife* (1864)

---

## Introduction: The First Indian Novel in English

Bankimchandra Chattopadhyay’s *Rajmohan’s Wife* (1864) is often called the **first Indian novel written in English**. Published in *The Indian Field*, a short-lived Calcutta magazine, it appeared at a moment of historical tension — only seven years after the Indian Rebellion of 1857 and three years before the publication of *Durgeshnandini* (1865), his first novel in Bengali.  

The novel’s position at the intersection of **colonial history, linguistic choice, and genre experiment** makes it a foundational text for understanding nineteenth-century literary globality. It raises questions about what it meant to write a “Victorian” novel from outside Britain, and how the novel form itself became a vehicle for negotiating empire, gender, and language.  

> **Mini-Essay — A Novel Between Worlds**  
> *Rajmohan’s Wife* sits at a threshold: between Bengali and English, between local realism and imported melodrama. Its hybrid form makes visible the cultural labour of writing under empire — a writer working *within* the language of power yet reshaping it from the margins.  

---

## Historical and Cultural Background

### Empire and the East India Company

The novel’s world is framed by the Bengal Presidency, the administrative heart of British India after the **Battle of Plassey (1757)**. The East India Company’s rule transformed trade into conquest; by 1858, after the Rebellion, control had passed to the British Crown. Chattopadhyay was born in 1838 into a Brahmin family near Calcutta, studied at the new University of Calcutta, and entered the **Indian Civil Service** — the very machinery of colonial governance.  

> “Because of the military successes of Cornwallis… Daniell and his nephew were the first of the artist travellers to be able to journey through all three Company Presidencies — Calcutta, Madras and Bombay.” (Hermione de Almeida & George Gilpin, *Indian Renaissance*, 2005):contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

This imperial infrastructure created the conditions for Chattopadhyay’s education, employment, and readership. Yet his English novel also exposes the fissures of that same system.  

### The Bengali Renaissance

Nineteenth-century Bengal saw a period of **intellectual and literary flowering**, sometimes called the “Bengal Renaissance.” Reform movements, missionary schools, and new print cultures produced a class of bilingual intellectuals — the *bhadralok* — who sought to reconcile traditional Hindu learning with Western modernity.  

Bankim, one of the first graduates of Calcutta University, embodied this bilingual modernity. His choice to write *Rajmohan’s Wife* in English aligns him with this *bhadralok* experiment: to speak back to empire in its own tongue while remaking that language for Indian experience.

## Genre, Form, and the Colonial Novel

### The Hybrid Aesthetic

The novel combines **melodrama, realism, and local reportage**, constantly shifting tone and genre. It begins in leisurely realism and ends in sensational haste. The narrative oscillates between omniscient commentary and intimate detail, revealing a consciousness of its own artifice:

> “We need not detain the reader with a detail of either the dialogue or the prattle… we will do him the justice to presume he has already some conception.”:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}  

Such self-conscious narration reveals a writer negotiating the conventions of the English novel while testing their applicability to Indian life.  

### The Sensational and the Real

Like Wilkie Collins or Bulwer Lytton, Bankim employs the tropes of **sensation fiction** — the dungeon, the abduction, the moral scandal. The story’s central triangle of Mahashvini (Matangini), her brutal husband Rajmohan, and her brother-in-law Madhav transforms domestic melodrama into a critique of patriarchal violence.  

Yet alongside this are moments of minute realism:  
> “His elder brother had wilfully left his inkpot behind… the child’s eyes had fallen on it, and he was joyfully smearing his face with the ink.”:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}  

These glimpses of everyday life — a child’s ink-smeared face, paan stains on walls, light falling on a veranda — create an intimacy alien to colonial ethnography. The novel’s realism is local, sensual, and particular.

> **Mini-Essay — The Double Texture of Style**  
> The collision of melodrama and domestic minutiae produces what we might call *vernacular realism*: a realism inflected by imported narrative structures but rooted in lived Bengali texture. Bankim turns the foreign form of the English novel into a vessel for regional experience.

---

## Language and Translation

The linguistic history of *Rajmohan’s Wife* is labyrinthine. The original English serial was lost; only fragments survive through **Bankim’s later Bengali self-translation** and **Brajendranath Banerjee’s 1935 re-translation into English**. The result is a palimpsest — a novel translated twice through its own languages.

An emblematic instance is the euphemism *Billingsgate*, describing Rajmohan’s abusive language:  
> “It is unnecessary to try the patience of my readers by reproducing all of his Billingsgate.”:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}  

In Bengali, the line reads: *“I won’t torture my readers’ ears by quoting those sweet words here.”* The English idiom, referring to London’s coarse fish-market slang, creates a startling imaginative bridge between **East Bengal and metropolitan England**.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Idiom of Empire**  
> The intrusion of “Billingsgate” into a Bengali scene performs linguistic colonialism in miniature: English idiom colonises the narrative voice. Yet it also stages a moment of translation — a writer and later translators actively choosing how to voice violence in a borrowed tongue.

## Nation, Empire, and the Texture of Place

### The Quiet Politics of Setting

The novel never directly addresses empire, yet colonial power seeps through every detail — from the Irish magistrate who resolves the plot to the European paintings on a zamindar’s wall:  

> “On the two remaining walls… might be seen arrayed a few specimens of European art, and the exquisite conception of the Virgin and Child.”:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}  

These objects possess “no symbolic weight”; their meaning lies in their indifference. They are relics of colonial presence absorbed into domestic habit.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Empire in the Room**  
> The Virgin and Child hang beneath the Hindu goddesses Kali and Durga, creating an iconographic collage of empire and devotion. Yet the narrator’s detachment — “the inmates… had little knowledge what the artist’s genius had strove to represent” — converts domination into decoration. The colonial object becomes simply another household thing, both sign of power and token of its irrelevance.

---

## The Regional and the Provincial

### From the Village to the City

*Rajmohan’s Wife* alternates between **East Bengal’s rural villages** and the **urban magnetism of Calcutta**, echoing Raymond Williams’s “country and city” dialectic. Mahashvini’s accent marks her as a girl “born and brought up near the capital” — a small but telling indicator of regional hierarchy.  

Ian Duncan’s account of the **provincial novel** helps situate Bankim’s experiment:  
> “The region is a place in itself, the source of its own terms of meaning and identity, while the province is a typical setting defined by its difference from London.”:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}  

By that definition, *Rajmohan’s Wife* constructs a **region**, not a province. Its Bengal is self-sufficient, with its own ecology of mud floors, bamboo fences, mango trees, and paan stains.  

> **Mini-Essay — Local Attachments**  
> The opening line — “There is a small village on the River Madhumati” — establishes presence rather than absence. Bankim’s geography is not derivative of empire; it is lived space, an ecology of touch and air. His realism thus challenges the London-centred vision of the Victorian novel, producing what we might call a *subcontinental provincialism* — local, but global in its awareness of being seen.

---

## Gender, the Jungle, and the Gothic

The novel’s central figure, **Matangini**, is both heroine and transgressor — a woman who defies her husband’s violence and ventures alone into the jungle at night. The jungle scene is one of the most striking in nineteenth-century colonial fiction:

> “At this hour of dread loneliness a young woman would have to thread her way through a wild and jungly path… the knotted trunks of huge trees showed like so many unearthly forms… each leafy bough… she fancied there lurked a demon.”:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}  

The passage blends Gothic terror with ecological precision: tamarind trees, reptiles, and night-birds create a sensory realism even as they evoke supernatural dread. Matangini’s escape — hiding in a dark pool, loosening her hair to disguise her pale face — fuses body and landscape.

> **Mini-Essay — The Feminine Sublime**  
> The jungle scene literalises Edmund Burke’s aesthetics of terror — “whatever is conversant about terrible objects is a source of the sublime” — but through a woman’s body. Matangini becomes both subject and object of the sublime, her agency born from fear. Her immersion in mud and water collapses distinctions between self and environment, purity and pollution, echoing Vaishnava devotional imagery where Radha meets Krishna in the forest.  

---

## Ecology and Etymology

The handout notes the Sanskrit origin of *jungle* (*jangala*, dry ground) against the Latin root of *forest* (*foris*, “outside”). This etymology itself mirrors colonial translation — a native word naturalised into English.  

In *Rajmohan’s Wife*, the jungle is not an alien wilderness but a **site of encounter and transformation**. It holds bandits, spirits, and lovers alike. By embedding the Gothic within a Bengali ecology, Bankim reclaims the wilderness from imperial exoticism.

---

## Conclusion: The World in a Village

In its hundred compressed pages, *Rajmohan’s Wife* remakes the nineteenth-century novel’s geography. The village of Radhaganj becomes a microcosm of empire, language, and gender. Its worldliness lies not in cosmopolitan sweep but in **intimate provincial depth** — a world literature of the small scale.

> **Mini-Essay — Provincial Modernity**  
> Where European world literature sought universality through circulation, Bankim achieves it through attention. His realism of mud, paan, and bamboo makes the local world legible without translation. The “first Indian novel in English” thus anticipates later theories of global modernity: the world refracted through the village, the universal born from the provincial.
