## Oscar Wilde: The Art of Biography and the Biography of Art  
### Prelims Paper 3: 1830–1910  

---

## Introduction: The Drama of Life and Art  

“Would you like to know the great drama of my life? It is that I have put my genius into my life – I have put only my talent into my works.”:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

Oscar Wilde’s famous boast captures the paradox of his reputation: his life has often eclipsed his art. From his trial and imprisonment to his dazzling epigrams, Wilde’s persona became the most enduring performance of his career. Yet his work — in its wit, irony, and aesthetic philosophy — continually reflects upon that performance, turning life into art and art into life.  

> **Mini-Essay — Wilde’s Self as Artwork**  
> Wilde treated identity as aesthetic material. His flamboyant persona was not a mask concealing truth but a creative act — “the supreme reality” through which life itself became art. His challenge to moral and artistic convention lay in this inversion: to live beautifully was the highest art form.  

---

## I. Multiple Wildes: Reception and Reinvention  

Wilde’s afterlife has been a battleground of interpretation. From early pathologising biographies to modern queer theory, critics have claimed many “true Wildes”:  

- **Wilde the Irishman** (Declan Kiberd, Jerusha McCormack, Jarlath Killeen) — colonial outsider, inheritor of Irish satire.  
- **Wilde the Gay Icon** (Christopher Craft, Neil McKenna) — pioneer of homosexual identity and aesthetics.  
- **Wilde the Socialist and Anarchist** (*The Soul of Man under Socialism*, 1891) — visionary of individualist ethics.  
- **Wilde the Catholic and Mystic** — preoccupied with redemption and suffering.  
- **Wilde the Professional Writer** (Josephine Guy and Ian Small) — pragmatic craftsman of the literary marketplace.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Spectator’s Mirror**  
> “It is the spectator, and not life, that art really mirrors” (*Preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray*). Wilde’s own critics become examples of this truth. Each version of Wilde reflects its interpreter’s desires. The richer the work, the more diverse the readings — “I pity that book on which critics are agreed” (Letter to W. E. Henley, 1888):contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}.  

---

## II. Masks and Mirrors: *Salomé* and the Politics of Looking  

In *Salomé* (1893), Wilde dramatises the act of **seeing as creation**. Each character describes the moon differently — corpse, virgin, madwoman — revealing more about themselves than about what they behold.  

> HEROD: “Neither at things, nor at people should one look. Only in mirrors should one look, for mirrors do but show us masks.”:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}  

Here Wilde rewrites the **myth of Medusa**: to look directly is to petrify, to destroy; to look through art — the mirror — is to transform. The play’s obsession with vision, desire, and control becomes a meditation on **subjectivity itself**.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Power of the Gaze**  
> For Wilde, perception is possession. To look is to create. *Salomé* turns spectatorship into seduction and art into danger: “the world is a reflection of who we think we are, mirrored back to us.” The mirror becomes both shield and snare — revelation through performance.

---

## III. Life as Performance  

Wilde’s life was as constructed as his prose. The celebrated photographs by Napoleon Sarony (1882) display a carefully choreographed aesthetic identity: the velvet coat, the languid pose, the “Professor of Aesthetics” touring America with lilies and wit.  

His dictum — *“the only duty we owe to history is to rewrite it”* — is embodied in his public persona. Wilde’s celebrity anticipates the **modern self as spectacle**, a self simultaneously authentic and artificial, both subject and object of desire.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Mask as Revelation**  
> Wilde’s paradoxes — “Give him a mask and he will tell you the truth” — overturn Romantic sincerity. The mask liberates rather than conceals. In performance, Wilde discovered a freedom unavailable to the moralised self.  

---

## IV. De Profundis: The Letter from Prison  

Written during his imprisonment in Reading Gaol (1895–97), Wilde’s *Epistola: In Carcere et Vinculis* (“Letter: In Prison and in Chains”) is both confession and creation — a self-portrait forged in suffering.  

### Composition and Transmission  
Wilde wrote an 80-page manuscript, addressed to Lord Alfred Douglas but intended for multiple readers. He instructed his friend Robert Ross to copy and preserve it “on good paper such as is used for plays, with wide rubricated margins”:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}. Wilde planned it as both private explanation and public testament — “a literary executor must be in possession of the only document that really explains my conduct.”  

Ross later published one-third of it in 1905 under the title **De Profundis** (“Out of the Depths”), suppressing references to Douglas. The full manuscript appeared only in 1962.  

> **Mini-Essay — The Text as Performance**  
> *De Profundis* is less a letter than a staged confession. Wilde’s request for margins and copies suggests he conceived it as a script — a play of repentance and resistance. It performs the transformation it describes: the artist remaking himself through art.  

---

## V. The Prison and the Mirror: Suffering as Self-Creation  

Wilde rejects the notion of a stable “true self.” Identity, like art, is process and play. Yet imprisonment forces a new realism upon him:  

> “I have got to make everything that has happened to me good for me…. There is not a single degradation of the body which I must not try and make into a spiritualising of the soul.”:contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}  

His self-diagnosis oscillates between irony and grandeur:  

> “Blindly I staggered as an ox to the shambles.”  
> “Your father will always live among the pure-minded parents of Sunday-school literature… and in the lowest mire of Malebolge I sit between Gilles de Rais and the Marquis de Sade.”:contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}  

The grotesque humour of these passages reveals Wilde’s refusal to sanctify suffering. His penitence is performative — an aesthetic of irony even in abasement.  

---

## VI. Art as Redemption  

In *De Profundis*, Wilde revises his earlier disdain for pity. Once a prophet of aesthetic detachment, he now discovers compassion through pain. He redefines Christ not as moral redeemer but as **the supreme artist of the self**:  

> “[Christ’s] chief war was against the Philistines.… The Jew of Jerusalem in Christ’s day was the exact counterpart of the British Philistine of our own.”:contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}  

Christ becomes the model of imaginative sympathy — “the artist of humanity” who finds beauty even in sin. For Wilde, art and religion converge in this creative empathy.  

> **Mini-Essay — Wilde’s Gospel of Art**  
> To create is to forgive. Wilde’s Christ loves through imagination, transforming judgment into art. His religion is aestheticism sanctified — beauty as the mode of moral truth.  

---

## VII. From Aestheticism to Anarchy  

Wilde’s early manifesto, *The Soul of Man under Socialism* (1891), had already envisioned the artist as revolutionary individualist. In prison, he extends this idea: suffering becomes the crucible of freedom.  

He imagines himself as both **Christ and Pope**, dictating to Ross:  

> “If the copying is done at Honiton Street, the lady type-writer might be fed through a lattice in the door like the Cardinals when they elect a Pope, till she comes out on the balcony and can say to the world *Habet Mundus Epistolam*.”:contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}  

The self-mockery here — half-sacred, half-satirical — epitomises Wilde’s **ironic sanctity**: martyrdom performed as theatre.  

> **Mini-Essay — Wilde as Pontiff of Paradox**  
> Wilde crowns himself Pope of Art, issuing an “encyclical letter” to humanity. The absurdity is deliberate: he parodies the very authority he claims, turning confession into ceremony, humiliation into authorship.  

---

## VIII. The Artist as Christ  

At the height of *De Profundis*, Wilde fuses autobiography with theology:  

> “I was a man who stood in symbolic relations to the art and culture of my age.… I treated Art as the supreme reality, and life as a mere mode of fiction.”:contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}  

In making himself emblematic — artist as sinner, sinner as saint — Wilde both exalts and critiques his society. His “war with the Philistines” becomes the modern Passion: the artist persecuted for beauty.  

---

## IX. Acceptance and Transcendence  

The letter’s closing pages achieve a serene defiance:  

> “Do not be afraid of the past. The past, the present and the future are but one moment in the sight of God.… A thing is according to the mode in which one looks at it.”:contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}  

Here Wilde recovers the aesthetic creed of perception as creation. What had been tragedy becomes art; what had been shame becomes vision.  

> **Mini-Essay — Redemption as Reinterpretation**  
> Wilde’s final act of self-creation is hermeneutic: he transforms biography into criticism. To “look with different eyes” is both repentance and revision — art as the rewriting of one’s own life.  

---

## X. Legacy and Critical Afterlives  

Wilde’s *De Profundis* bridges Victorian morality and modernist self-consciousness. Its hybrid form — letter, sermon, autobiography, manifesto — anticipates twentieth-century explorations of **the self as text**.  

Later critics read Wilde as:  
- **Queer theorist avant la lettre** (Ellis Hanson, Alan Sinfield) — identity as performance.  
- **Political aesthete** (Regina Gagnier) — art as social critique.  
- **Martyr of modernism** (T. S. Eliot, W. H. Auden) — suffering transfigured into art.  

The Oxford English Texts edition of *De Profundis* (ed. Ian Small, 2005) now distinguishes between *De Profundis* (Ross’s version) and *Epistola: In Carcere et Vinculis* (the full manuscript), preserving Wilde’s multiple selves.
