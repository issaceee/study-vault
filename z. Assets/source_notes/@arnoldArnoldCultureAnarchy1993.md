> The whole scope of the essay is to recommend culture as the great help out of our present difficulties; ==culture being a pursuit of our total perfection by means of getting to know, on all the matters which most concern us, the best which has been thought and said in the world, and, through this knowledge, turning a stream of fresh and free thought upon our stock notions and habits, which we now follow staunchly but mechanically, vainly imagining that there is a virtue in following them staunchly which makes up for the mischief of following them mechanically.== This, and this alone, is the scope of the following essay. I say again here, what I have said in the pages which follow, that from the faults and weaknesses of bookmen a notion of something bookish, pedantic, and futile has got itself more or less connected with the word culture, and that it is a pity we cannot use a word more perfectly free from all shadow of reproach. And yet, futile as are many bookmen, and helpless as books and reading often prove for bringing nearer to perfection those who [ix] use them, one must, I think, be struck more and more, the longer one lives, to find how much, in our present society, a man's life of each day depends for its solidity and value on whether he reads during that day, and, far more still, on what he reads during it. More and more he who examines himself will find the difference it makes to him, at the end of any given day, whether or no he has pursued his avocations throughout it without reading at all; and whether or no, having read something, he has read the newspapers only. This, however, is a matter for each man's private conscience and experience. If a man without books or reading, or reading nothing but his letters and the newspapers, gets nevertheless a fresh and free play of the best thoughts upon his stock notions and habits, he has got culture. He has got that for which we prize and recommend culture; he has got that which at the present moment we seek culture that it may give us. This inward operation is the very life and essence of culture, as we conceive it.


Arnold argues that detractors think that culture is unintellactual and unacademic - just mere simple curiosuty or done out of vanity or exclusiveness
for the English, culture is always tainted with a sense ofunintellectual and frivolous activity (curiousity)
while there is frivolous curiosity, there is also a sense of curiosity in which one strives to learn for learning sake, which requires effort and contrary to the frivolous perspective
in addition to the scientific motive of curiosity, culture is also motivated socially - in our want to make things better for others and society as a whole, perfecting our world
Culture is different from Religion in the sense that Culture acts on both curiosity and the strive of perfection. where the latter strives to act rather than think, Culture balances both aspects as it understands that "acting and instituting are of little use, unless we know how and what we ought to act and to institute"
this culture needs to be situated within a period of intellectual paradigm change, in which we are discovering new things every day
in this new parradigm, our previous rules and routines are broken done, allowing for a this strive for culture, the strive for perfection.
